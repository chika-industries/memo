### Scene 4: Amortization

**Setting:** Jerry's apartment. Jerry is on the couch. Kramer enters, holding a large, wrapped sub sandwich.

**Kramer:** Jerry! You're witnessing a financial experiment!

**Jerry:** Is the experiment to see if you can finish that sub before your heart gives out?

**Kramer:** No! This sub cost me $12. Now, I could eat it all now, and enjoy $12 worth of instant gratification. But that's not smart accounting.

**Jerry:** Eating a sandwich is usually the smart accounting move, yes.

**Kramer:** No! I'm going to amortize it!

**Jerry:** You're going to what?

**Kramer:** Amortize! It's what companies do with big expenses! They spread the cost out over time! So, I'm not going to eat this whole sub today. I'm going to eat one quarter of it today. Then tomorrow, another quarter. Spreading the cost, and the enjoyment, over four days! That's amortization!

**Jerry:** So... you're going to eat a four-day-old sandwich.

**Kramer:** A *strategically aged* sandwich! By day four, the flavors will have really... amortized together.

**Jerry:** Kramer, the flavors won't amortize. The lettuce will liquefy. The bread will become a science experiment. You're not amortizing the cost, you're just eating a really sad, old sandwich.

**Kramer:** No, no. I'm matching the expense with the period of benefit, Jerry! That's the principle! I benefit from this sandwich for four days, so the cost should be spread over four days! It's fundamentally sound!

**Jerry:** The benefit you'll get on day four is a one-way ticket to the bathroom. You're gonna be amortizing all right, but it won't be a sandwich. Trust me.
