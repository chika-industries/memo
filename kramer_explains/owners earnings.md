### Scene 17: Owner's Earnings (The Buffett Special)

**Setting:** Monk's Diner. Jerry is reading a newspaper. George enters, carrying a well-worn book with visible excitement.

**George:** Jerry! I've found it! The holy grail! The secret to actual wealth, not this unrealized nonsense Kramer keeps pushing!

**Jerry:** What is that? A cookbook? Because you look hungry for something.

**George:** *(Slamming book on table)* "The Essays of Warren Buffett"! The Oracle of Omaha! The Sage of—whoever! This guy knows everything, Jerry! And he talks about something called "owner's earnings"!

**Jerry:** Owner's earnings. That sounds like what a landlord calls the rent he collects while he does nothing.

**George:** No, no! It's deeper! Buffett says you can't trust reported earnings because companies have to spend money just to *stay* in business! You have to calculate what's actually left for the owner after all the *necessary* stuff!

**Jerry:** So it's like... real earnings. After accounting for the things you *have* to do.

**George:** Exactly! You take net income, add back depreciation and amortization—Kramer taught me that one—but then you subtract the *capital expenditures* you need just to maintain your competitive position! That's owner's earnings!

*(Kramer bursts in, naturally.)*

**Kramer:** Did someone say Buffett? I love that guy! He drinks Cherry Coke and knows everything! I once ate a See's Candy and felt spiritually connected to Omaha!

**George:** Kramer! You're just in time! We're talking about owner's earnings! This is the real deal! No more unrealized fantasy gains!

**Kramer:** Owner's earnings! Of course! I've been calculating mine wrong all along! I've been counting gross revenue from hat sales—

**Jerry:** Which is zero.

**Kramer:** —without subtracting my maintenance CapEx!

**Jerry:** Your what?

**Kramer:** Capital expenditures for maintenance! To keep my hat business going, I have to spend money! On storage! On dusting supplies! On those little moisture absorber packets so the hats don't get mildew! That's maintenance CapEx, Jerry! It doesn't show up on a regular income statement, but it's real!

**Jerry:** You buy moisture absorber packets for hats that never leave your apartment?

**Kramer:** A good businessman maintains his inventory! You think Buffett lets his See's Candies get moldy? No! He invests in quality control!

**George:** This is exactly what I'm talking about! Take my situation. My income is $400 a week from the real estate office. But to maintain my position as an employee, I have to spend money on commuting, on dry cleaning, on buying coffee to fit in with the other agents—

**Jerry:** You buy coffee to fit in? You've been stealing coffee from the break room for 15 years.

**George:** That's my point! My maintenance CapEx is artificially low because I'm a thief! My owner's earnings are actually *higher* than they should be because I'm cutting corners! That's smart business!

**Kramer:** He's right! The lower your maintenance costs, the higher your owner's earnings! It's about efficiency!

**Jerry:** It's about theft and neglecting basic business practices. That's not efficiency, that's just being cheap and lazy.

---

**Setting:** Jerry's apartment. Later that day. George is furiously writing on a napkin. Kramer is pacing.

**George:** Okay, I've calculated my owner's earnings. Starting with my gross salary of $20,800 per year.

**Jerry:** That's depressing just to hear.

**George:** Add back depreciation... I depreciate my car at $500 per year even though it's actually falling apart faster than that, so I'm being conservative—

**Jerry:** You can't just make up depreciation on yourself. You're not a company asset.

**George:** I'm an asset to my employer! I depreciate daily! It's real!

**Kramer:** Let him work, Jerry! This is advanced finance!

**George:** Then subtract maintenance CapEx. I spend approximately $300 per year on basic George upkeep—haircuts, deodorant, the occasional meal that isn't stolen from my parents—

**Jerry:** $300 for the whole year? That's $25 a month. You can't maintain a human on $25 a month.

**George:** I'm low-maintenance! It's a competitive advantage! My owner's earnings are $20,500! That's what I truly generate for myself as an economic unit!

**Kramer:** That's beautiful, George. Truly beautiful. Now watch this. *(Pulls out his own napkin)* Kramerica Hat Industries: Projected annual revenue: $12,000.

**Jerry:** Projected from what? A parallel universe?

**Kramer:** From future sales! Now, add back depreciation: my hats lose value over time, but on paper I add that back! That's $2,000 of depreciation add-back!

**Jerry:** You can't add back depreciation on hats you haven't sold. That's not how any of this works.

**Kramer:** Now subtract maintenance CapEx: hat storage, hat cleaning supplies, the fan I run to keep them ventilated—$800 per year. My owner's earnings? $13,200!

**George:** That's incredible! Your business generates more owner's earnings than my salary!

**Kramer:** That's the power of entrepreneurship, George! Buffett would be proud!

**Jerry:** Buffett would be baffled. You've made up revenue, invented depreciation, and underestimated your expenses. Your owner's earnings are imaginary. George's are just depressing. Neither of you is even close to what Buffett means.

*(Newman appears at the door.)*

**Newman:** Did I hear someone say "owner's earnings"? Because as a postal employee, I'm intimately familiar with the concept. My route generates $65,000 in gross revenue for the USPS, but after maintenance CapEx—fuel, vehicle repairs, the constant emotional toll of dealing with dogs—my personal owner's earnings are approximately $38,000.

**Jerry:** Oh great, now everyone's a Buffett disciple.

**Newman:** The difference between me and these two amateurs is that I actually understand *sustainable* owner's earnings. Kramer, your hat business has negative owner's earnings because your maintenance CapEx exceeds your revenue. George, your owner's earnings are positive only because you're a mooch who doesn't pay for basic necessities. Neither model is sustainable.

**Kramer:** Negative owner's earnings? Is that possible?

**Newman:** It means you're losing value every day you stay in business. You're not generating wealth—you're consuming it.

**George:** *(Stunned)* So I'm... okay? Because mine are positive?

**Newman:** Yours are positive but fragile. One unexpected expense—a medical bill, a car repair, a day when you actually have to buy lunch—and you're underwater. You have no margin of safety.

**Jerry:** Margin of safety. Another Buffett term. These guys are getting a full education today.

**Kramer:** So what do we do? How do we increase our owner's earnings?

**Newman:** Kramer, you need to either sell hats or stop buying them. Those are your options. George, you need to either increase your revenue or reduce your personal maintenance CapEx—which, frankly, is already at starvation levels.

**George:** I can't reduce my maintenance! I'm already at the minimum viable human level! One less haircut and I look like a cave person!

**Kramer:** And I can't stop buying hats! The inventory is the business!

**Newman:** Then you're both trapped. Your owner's earnings are what they are. Accept your financial fates.

*(Newman exits smugly. Kramer and George slump.)*

**Jerry:** You know, for once, Newman was actually right. Your owner's earnings are pathetic, but they're honest. That's more than you usually have.

**George:** So I'm pathetic but honest? That's my Buffett thesis?

**Jerry:** It's a start. Now if you could somehow increase revenue without increasing expenses—

**Kramer:** That's it! We need revenue! George, you work at a real estate office! You have access to wealthy people! Sell them hats!

**George:** Sell my clients hats? "Congratulations on your new home, here's a feathered monstrosity from 1985"?

**Kramer:** They're vintage! They're statement pieces! Every home needs a statement piece!

**Jerry:** The only statement those hats make is "I've given up on interior design."

**George:** *(Thinking)* Wait... what if I don't sell them hats... what if I sell them *me*? As a consultant? "George Costanza, Hat Consultant for the Modern Home"?

**Kramer:** That's brilliant! You consult, I supply! We split the owner's earnings 50-50!

**Jerry:** You're going to consult on hat placement? In people's homes? You don't even have furniture. You sit on a pile of mail at your parents' house.

**George:** That's what makes me relatable! I'm a man of the people! I understand hat adjacency!

**Kramer:** This could be huge, Jerry! Costanza-Kramer Hat Advisory Services! Owner's earnings through the roof!

**Jerry:** Your owner's earnings will be through the roof of a very small building. But go ahead. I want to see how this unfolds.

---

**Setting:** Monk's Diner. One month later. George and Kramer sit across from Jerry, looking defeated but somehow proud.

**Jerry:** Let me guess. Hat consulting didn't take off.

**George:** We had one client. One.

**Kramer:** A lovely woman with too much money and not enough taste.

**George:** She paid us $200 to come to her apartment and arrange hats on her walls as "sculptural elements."

**Kramer:** It was a triumph, Jerry. We placed 12 hats. The lobster one was the centerpiece.

**Jerry:** So you made $200. That's revenue. What were your expenses?

**George:** Transportation: $15. Snacks we bought to look professional: $12. And... the hats themselves.

**Kramer:** We used prime inventory. High-quality pieces. The lobster hat alone was worth at least $50.

**Jerry:** So you "spent" hats you already had, that you couldn't sell anyway, at valuations you made up, to calculate your profit?

**George:** That's the beauty part! Our maintenance CapEx was zero! We used existing assets! So our owner's earnings on this transaction were the full $200 minus $27 in out-of-pocket expenses! That's $173!

**Kramer:** And we split it! $86.50 each! That's pure owner's earnings!

**Jerry:** So you each made $86.50. After a month. And you used up 12 hats that you'll never get back.

**George:** But we *realized* value from them! For the first time ever, the hats generated cash!

**Kramer:** It's a new era, Jerry! The hat business is finally producing owner's earnings!

**Jerry:** You know what Buffett would say about this?

**George:** What?

**Jerry:** He'd say you had a good week. But he'd also ask about next week, and the week after, and whether you can do this consistently without destroying your inventory. That's the difference between a transaction and a business.

**Kramer:** *(Pause)* ...So we need more clients.

**George:** And more hats.

**Kramer:** Actually, we need the same hats, but we need to get them back from the clients so we can reuse them.

**George:** A hat rental model! That's even better!

**Jerry:** You're going to knock on that woman's door and ask for the lobster hat back so you can rent it to someone else?

**Kramer:** It's called asset utilization, Jerry! Maximizing owner's earnings!

**George:** This could be bigger than hats! This could be... art rental! Furniture rental! George rental!

**Jerry:** George rental? People can rent you?

**George:** For parties! For events! "Want a guy who will argue about anything and eat your food? Rent George Costanza!"

**Kramer:** I'm in! We'll be a rental empire! Costanza-Kramer Rental Solutions!

**Jerry:** Your owner's earnings are about to become owner's losses. But I'll watch. It's the least I can do.

*(George and Kramer exit, already arguing about who gets what percentage of the rental revenue.)*

**Jerry:** *(To himself)* Buffett also talks about circles of competence. These two aren't just outside their circle—they're in a completely different dimension.
