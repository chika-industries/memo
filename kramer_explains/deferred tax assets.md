
### Scene 18: Deferred Tax Assets

**Setting:** Jerry's apartment. Jerry is at the kitchen counter. George bursts in, holding a letter, looking panicked.

**George:** Jerry! The IRS! They're onto me! I'm finished! I'm gonna end up in a cell with a guy who communicates entirely through coughs!

**Jerry:** What did you do now? Claim your mother as a dependent? Because emotionally, that's accurate, but tax-wise...

**George:** No, no! It's worse! I carried forward my losses! And now they want to know about them!

**Jerry:** You carried forward losses? From what? You don't have a business. You have a job. A bad one.

**George:** I have a side hustle! Remember when I tried to sell t-shirts with "I'm not a Costanza" on them? Total disaster! Lost $800!

**Jerry:** You sold seven shirts. To your cousins. At a discount.

**George:** Exactly! Business losses! And according to tax law, I can carry those losses forward to offset future profits! That's a deferred tax asset, Jerry! I have an asset that the IRS owes me!

*(Kramer bursts in, naturally.)*

**Kramer:** Deferred tax assets! I was just reading about those! It's when the government says, "You screwed up so bad this year that we'll let you screw up less in the future!" It's a forgiveness asset!

**Jerry:** That's not... it's not forgiveness. It's just matching losses against future gains so you don't get double-taxed on nothing.

**George:** Whatever it is, I have one! $800 in losses carried forward! That means when I eventually make money—and I will, Jerry, I will—I won't have to pay taxes on the first $800!

**Jerry:** That's assuming you make money. That's assuming a lot.

**Kramer:** I have one too! Bigger than George's! My hat business has accumulated losses of approximately $4,000 over the past year!

**Jerry:** That's not a deferred tax asset. That's just regular debt and sadness.

**Kramer:** No, no! I've been tracking! Every hat I don't sell, I record as a loss! Every umbrella that sits in the corner, another loss! I'm building up a massive deferred tax asset! When the hat market turns—and it will, Jerry, it will—I'll have all these losses to offset my massive profits!

**Jerry:** You're recording inventory that hasn't sold as a loss? That's not how it works! Losses happen when you sell something for less than you paid, or when your expenses exceed your revenue. You can't just look at a hat and say "that's a loss" and claim it on your taxes!

**Kramer:** Why not? It's losing value every day! That's economic loss! The tax code should recognize reality!

**George:** He's got a point, Jerry. My t-shirts are still sitting in my parents' basement. That's inventory that never sold! That's a loss!

**Jerry:** You have seven t-shirts with a terrible slogan. That's not inventory, that's evidence of bad decisions. There's no tax deduction for being embarrassing.

---

**Setting:** Monk's Diner. George and Kramer are at a booth, both with notebooks, deeply engaged in calculation. Jerry sips coffee and watches.

**George:** Okay, so if my deferred tax asset is $800, and my tax rate is roughly 20%, that means I have $160 of future tax savings!

**Kramer:** Mine's bigger. $4,000 in losses at 20% is $800 in future savings! I'm richer than you in deferred terms!

**George:** But my losses are real! Yours are hats you made up!

**Kramer:** They're both real on paper! That's the beauty! The IRS doesn't know which losses are real and which are... creatively documented!

**Jerry:** The IRS knows everything. That's literally their only job. They have computers and auditors and people who went to school for this. You two have crayons and hope.

*(Newman slides into the booth uninvited.)*

**Newman:** Deferred tax assets. Fascinating topic. I happen to have one myself.

**Jerry:** Of course you do.

**Newman:** As a postal employee, I incur significant uniform expenses. The shorts, Jerry. The *shorts*. They're a professional necessity and a personal humiliation. That's a deductible expense. But some years, my deductions exceed my income—

**George:** You have losses? A mailman with losses?

**Newman:** I had a bad year in the Beanie Baby market. Don't judge. The point is, I carried forward those losses. I have a deferred tax asset. Unlike you two, mine is legitimate.

**Kramer:** Legitimate, shmegitimate. The IRS accepts all losses equally.

**Newman:** The IRS accepts documented losses from actual business activities. George, your t-shirt venture was a hobby at best. Kramer, your hat "business" has never made a sale. Neither of you has a valid deferred tax asset. You have wishful thinking.

**George:** *(Deflating)* So my $800 in losses is worthless?

**Newman:** For tax purposes? Completely. For life purposes? It's a reminder of your poor judgment. That's not an asset, that's a memory.

**Kramer:** What about my hats? They're real! I have receipts!

**Newman:** You have receipts for purchasing inventory. That's not a loss until you sell them at a loss or they become worthless. Have you formally abandoned the hats? Donated them? Destroyed them?

**Kramer:** Destroy them? They're my children!

**Newman:** Then they're still assets. Not losses. Your deferred tax asset is an illusion.

*(Kramer and George slump. Jerry smirks.)*

**Jerry:** So let me get this straight. George has no deferred tax asset because his "business" was a joke. Kramer has no deferred tax asset because he won't admit his hats are worthless. Newman has one because he lost money on Beanie Babies while wearing official shorts. This is the most pathetic tax conversation in human history.

**George:** So what do I do? Just accept that I have no future tax benefit from my suffering?

**Kramer:** There has to be a way! What if we combine our losses? Form a partnership and aggregate our deferred tax assets?

**Newman:** That's not how it works. You can't just pool imaginary losses.

**Jerry:** Although "Imaginary Losses Pool" would be a great name for a band.

**George:** *(Suddenly inspired)* Wait! What about *valuation allowances*? I read about this! If it's more likely than not that you won't realize your deferred tax asset, you have to establish a valuation allowance! That means you're officially saying your losses are worthless!

**Kramer:** Why would we do that? That's admitting defeat!

**George:** No, no! It's conservative accounting! It shows we're being honest! Then later, if things turn around, we can reverse the valuation allowance and recognize the asset!

**Jerry:** So you want to officially declare your losses worthless now so you can unofficially hope they're valuable later?

**George:** It's the Generally Accepted Accounting Principles, Jerry! It's what the professionals do!

**Kramer:** George is right! We need to be realistic! I'm gonna establish a 100% valuation allowance against my hat-related deferred tax assets!

**Jerry:** You can't establish an allowance against something that doesn't exist. It's like putting a "do not enter" sign on a door that leads to a wall.

**Newman:** For once, Jerry is correct. You can't take a valuation allowance against nothing. You first need a valid deferred tax asset. Which you don't have.

**George:** So we're in a catch-22? We need the asset to declare it worthless, but we can't have the asset because we don't have real losses?

**Jerry:** Welcome to tax accounting. It's almost as complicated as your lives, but with fewer hats.

---

**Setting:** Kramer's apartment. Later. George is sitting on a hat pile. Kramer paces.

**George:** I've been thinking. What if we create a business specifically designed to generate real losses? Legitimate ones? Then we'd have actual deferred tax assets!

**Kramer:** Like what? A business that's guaranteed to fail?

**George:** Exactly! Something that loses money predictably! Then we carry forward those losses, and when we eventually do something profitable—anything, really—we have the asset ready!

**Kramer:** That's genius! It's like... intentional failure for future benefit!

**George:** What could we do that's guaranteed to lose money?

**Kramer:** *(Pauses)* ...A restaurant?

**George:** Too expensive. We need something small-scale. Something that loses money consistently but not catastrophically.

**Kramer:** A lemonade stand? But we charge too little?

**George:** People might still buy it. We need something nobody wants.

*(They both look at each other.)*

**Kramer:** ...Hats?

**George:** No, we already have hats. Something new. Something even less desirable.

**Kramer:** There's nothing less desirable than hats.

**George:** *(Slowly)* ...What about... a blog?

**Kramer:** A blog? About what?

**George:** About nothing! About our lives! Nobody would read it! We'd pay for hosting, domain names, maybe some promotional ads—all expenses, zero revenue! Perfect losses!

**Kramer:** George, you're a genius! We'll call it "The Costanza-Kramer Chronicles"! We'll write about our daily experiences! Our struggles! Our financial wisdom!

**Jerry:** *(Appearing in the doorway)* Your financial wisdom is why you need losses in the first place. This is a self-licking ice cream cone of failure.

**George:** Jerry! You're just in time! We're starting a blog to generate tax losses!

**Jerry:** You're going to start a blog about your lives, lose money on it, and then claim those losses as deferred tax assets against future profits from... what, exactly?

**Kramer:** From the blog itself! Eventually it'll take off! Then we'll have profits and losses from the same activity! Net zero, but with timing differences! Deferred tax assets and liabilities canceling out!

**Jerry:** You've invented a way to lose money now so you can not pay taxes later on money you haven't made from the same thing that lost money. That's not tax planning. That's a circle. Again.

**George:** It's a tax strategy circle! They're the best kind!

**Kramer:** We're gonna start writing today! First post: "Why Hats Are the Future of Headwear"!

**George:** Second post: "I'm Not a Costanza: The T-Shirt Postmortem"!

**Jerry:** Third post: "We Have No Money and We're Writing About It." I'd read that one, actually.

**Newman:** *(Appearing behind Jerry)* I'll be a guest contributor. "Beanie Babies: A Cautionary Tale in Shorts."

**Jerry:** This is the worst blog in history and it hasn't even launched yet.

**Kramer:** That's the beauty! Maximum loss potential!

*(They all start brainstorming blog ideas. Jerry watches, shaking his head.)*

**Jerry:** You know what your deferred tax asset is worth?

**George:** What?

**Jerry:** Zero. Because you'll never have taxable profits. You'll just have an endless cycle of losses and bad ideas. The IRS doesn't owe you anything. You owe the universe about 20 years of productive work.

**Kramer:** *(Ignoring him)* George! What if we make the blog a subscription model? Then we have losses *and* nobody subscribes! Double the loss!

**George:** Kramer, you're a visionary!

**Jerry:** And I'm a witness. To a crime against finance.

*(Jerry exits as the group continues planning their money-losing blog.)*

**George:** *(Calling after him)* First month free for you, Jerry! That's a promotional expense!

**Jerry:** *(From hallway)* Put it on my tab. The one that doesn't exist.
