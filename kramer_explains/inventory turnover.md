Fantastic! Let's keep rolling through the Generally Accepted Kramer Principles (GAKP).

### Scene 8: Inventory Turnover

**Setting:** Kramer's apartment. It's absolutely filled to the brim with boxes of women's hats. Like, floor-to-ceiling, you can barely move.

**Setting:** Jerry's apartment. Kramer bursts in, but he has to turn sideways because he's carrying three more boxes.

**Kramer:** Jerry! You won't believe the deal I got!

**Jerry:** What are those? Boxes of... disappointment?

**Kramer:** Hats! Women's fancy hats! From the 1980s! A whole warehouse full! The guy was liquidating and I swooped in! Paid $200 for the whole lot!

**Jerry:** Kramer, there are thousands of hats here. Why do you have thousands of 1980s women's hats?

**Kramer:** Because, my friend, inventory turnover! It's the secret to retail success! You buy low, you sell fast, you turn that inventory over into cash! I'm gonna be a hat mogul!

**Jerry:** Sell fast to who? Who is clamoring for hats that Joan Collins wore on Dynasty?

**Kramer:** Everyone! It's vintage! It's retro! It's coming back! I'm gonna set up a cart on the street. "Kramer's Kustom Kovers" — no, "Kramer's Krowning Glory" — and I'm gonna turn this inventory over so fast, people's heads will spin!

**Jerry:** Okay, so let's say you sell one hat. How much profit?

**Kramer:** I paid maybe 20 cents a hat. I'll sell 'em for $20 each. That's a 10,000% markup, Jerry! My turnover ratio is gonna be through the roof!

**Jerry:** That's a great margin, but you have to actually *sell* them. How many have you sold so far?

**Kramer:** *(Pauses)* ...I put one on Newman's head to see how it looked.

**Jerry:** And?

**Kramer:** He wouldn't give it back. Said it made him feel "regal." So that's one gone, I guess. But he didn't pay for it, so it's more of a... promotional expense.

**Jerry:** So your inventory turnover so far is zero. Your apartment is a hat museum. And you're calculating profits based on sales that haven't happened.

**Kramer:** It's early days, Jerry! The hats need time to... marinate in the market. Once people see them, the turnover will happen. It's like a merry-go-round of commerce!

**Jerry:** It's like a merry-go-round of madness. You have to *store* these things. You have to *transport* them. You have to *market* them. You can't just buy a mountain of ugly hats and wait for the world to beat a path to your door.

**Kramer:** Why not? Field of Dreams, Jerry! "If you build it, they will come." Well, if I stack it, they will shop it!

**Jerry:** That's not how the quote goes. At all.

*(Suddenly, Newman bursts into Kramer's apartment across the hall. They hear a crash and then Newman appears in Jerry's doorway, wearing a large, feathered 80s hat.)*

**Newman:** Kramer! There's no room to sit! The hats are attacking! One of them fell on the stove and the feathers are smoldering!

**Kramer:** *(Gasps)* My inventory! The turnover is happening! In a bad way!

**Jerry:** That's not turnover. That's called "shrinkage." And I think you're about to write off the whole lot.

*(Kramer runs out. Newman adjusts his hat.)*

**Newman:** How do I look?

**Jerry:** Like a very lost peacock. Get out.

### Scene 9: Liquidity

**Setting:** Monk's Diner. Jerry and Elaine are having lunch. Kramer slides into the booth, looking desperate.

**Kramer:** Jerry. Elaine. I need help. I'm in a liquidity crisis.

**Elaine:** You're in a what now?

**Kramer:** Liquidity! It's when you have plenty of stuff, but no cash! I'm asset-rich and cash-poor!

**Jerry:** What assets do you have? The hats? Because last I checked, the hats were becoming one with your apartment.

**Kramer:** The hats are a big part of it, yes. Also, I have a signed photo of Mickey Mantle that I found in a dumpster. And a complete set of encyclopedias from 1974. And a broken exercise bike that I'm gonna fix any day now.

**Elaine:** So you have garbage.

**Kramer:** No! I have *assets*! But they're not *liquid*! I can't pay my rent with a signed photo of Mickey Mantle! Well, maybe I could, if I found the right Mickey Mantle fan. But my landlord is not that fan. He's a Yankees fan, but he's more of a Reggie Jackson guy.

**Jerry:** So your problem is you have things that are hard to turn into cash quickly without losing value. That's literally the definition of illiquidity.

**Kramer:** Exactly! I need to become more liquid! I need to convert some of these assets into spendable cash!

**Elaine:** So... sell the hats.

**Kramer:** I tried! I stood outside for three hours yesterday. Nobody wants hats! One woman said they reminded her of her grandmother's funeral. Another guy said they looked like "feathered nightmares."

**Jerry:** So the market has spoken. The hats are worth zero.

**Kramer:** No, no, no! They're worth something! I just need to find the right buyer! It's a liquidity problem, not a value problem!

**Elaine:** Kramer, if you can't sell something for what you think it's worth, it's worth what someone will pay for it. And if no one will pay anything, it's worth zero.

**Kramer:** That's insane! That's like saying a painting in a museum is only worth what someone would pay for it! What about the artistic value? The cultural significance?

**Jerry:** What's the cultural significance of a hat shaped like a lobster?

**Kramer:** It's... it's whimsical! It represents the carefree spirit of the 1980s! The excess! The big hair! The...

**Elaine:** The fact that nobody wants it in 2026.

**Kramer:** *(Slumping)* I'm gonna be evicted. They're gonna put my hats on the street. The lobster hat will be homeless.

**Jerry:** See, now *that's* a liquidity crisis. When your hats face eviction before you do.

**Kramer:** *(Suddenly brightening)* Wait a minute! What if I use the hats as collateral for a loan? I'll go to the bank, show them my inventory, and get a cash advance against my assets!

**Jerry:** You're going to walk into a bank with a box of 80s hats and ask for a loan.

**Kramer:** It's called asset-based lending, Jerry! It's how the rich stay rich!

**Elaine:** It's how Kramer stays homeless. Go ahead. Film it. I want to see the security guard's face.

**Kramer:** *(Standing up, determined)* I'm going to the bank. The lobster hat is coming with me. He's my good luck charm.

*(Kramer exits. A moment later, he comes back in, grabs a hat off his head that they didn't even notice he was wearing, and exits again.)*

**Jerry:** He was wearing a hat the whole time.

**Elaine:** A green one. With netting.

**Jerry:** I thought it was just... part of him. Like his hair had evolved.

### Scene 10: EBITDA

**Setting:** Jerry's apartment. Kramer bursts in, holding a crumpled Wall Street Journal and pointing at it excitedly.

**Kramer:** Jerry! I've found it! The holy grail! The number that explains everything!

**Jerry:** Is it the number of times you've burst through my door? Because we're in the thousands.

**Kramer:** No! EBITDA! Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization! It's how they measure the *real* performance of a company! Strip away all the noise and look at the pure operating earnings!

**Jerry:** Okay... and how does this apply to you?

**Kramer:** I've calculated my personal EBITDA for last month!

**Jerry:** You pay interest? You have loans?

**Kramer:** I borrowed $20 from Newman. That's interest-adjacent.

**Jerry:** You pay taxes?

**Kramer:** Not if I can help it! But that's the point, Jerry! You *exclude* them! You look at the core business!

**Jerry:** What is your core business?

**Kramer:** *(Pauses)* ...Living? Being Kramer? It's a lifestyle brand, really.

**Jerry:** Fine. What's your EBITDA?

**Kramer:** *(Pulls out a napkin - his official accounting document)* Okay. My earnings before everything... I made $300 last month!

**Jerry:** From what?

**Kramer:** I returned a bunch of bottles. Found $20 on the street. Helped an old lady with her groceries—she actually gave me $5 that time, cash money. And I sold one of the hats to a homeless guy for $2.

**Jerry:** You sold a hat to a homeless guy? What did he need with a fancy 80s hat?

**Kramer:** He said it kept the sun off. So, $300 in gross Kramer earnings!

**Jerry:** That's not $300. That's like $27.

**Kramer:** I'm rounding up! It's EBITDA, Jerry, it's not supposed to be precise! It's a vibe!

**Jerry:** A vibe.

**Kramer:** Now, I subtract nothing! Because interest, taxes, depreciation, amortization—I don't have any of that! So my EBITDA is $300! I'm killing it!

**Jerry:** You don't have any of those things because you don't have any of the things that cause them. You have no debt, so no interest. You make no money, so no taxes. You own nothing that depreciates except maybe your body, and that's definitely happening. And amortization? You've never even said that word correctly until today.

**Kramer:** So what you're saying is... my EBITDA is pure! It's untainted! It's the truest measure of my financial genius!

**Jerry:** No. What I'm saying is you made $27 last month and you're celebrating like you're Warren Buffett.

**Kramer:** Warren Buffett started somewhere, Jerry. He probably had a good EBITDA month too. I'm on my way! Next month, I'm gonna start a business. A real one. With EBITDA potential.

**Jerry:** What business?

**Kramer:** I'm thinking about becoming a professional line-stander. You know, for people who don't want to wait in line for restaurants or movie openings? I stand in line for them. Pure service revenue. High EBITDA margin. Low capital requirements.

**Jerry:** That's actually not the worst idea you've ever had.

**Kramer:** See? EBITDA! It sparks creativity!

**Jerry:** It sparks something. I just don't know if it's creativity or just more chaos.

**Kramer:** *(Heading for the door)* I'm gonna go stand in line at that new bakery. Get some practice in. Build that EBITDA!

*(Kramer exits. Jerry stares at the door.)*

**Jerry:** He's gonna stand in line for a bakery that he's not even going to buy anything from. That's not EBITDA. That's just... standing.
