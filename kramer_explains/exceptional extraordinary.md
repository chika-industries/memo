
### Scene 19: Extraordinary and Exceptional Items

**Setting:** Jerry's apartment. George is pacing, holding a laptop. Kramer is at the door, holding a receipt.

**George:** Jerry, we have a problem. The blog—"The Costanza-Kramer Chronicles"—it's hemorrhaging money. But not the good kind, the kind that actually costs real dollars.

**Jerry:** There's a good kind of hemorrhaging?

**George:** For tax purposes! But this is different. The hosting bill came. The domain renewal. And Kramer bought a "premium theme" for $80 that looks exactly like the free one.

**Kramer:** It has a gradient, Jerry! Gradients cost money!

**Jerry:** Apparently $80.

**George:** The question is: how do we report these losses? Are they part of our operating performance? Or are they something else?

**Kramer:** *(Slamming receipt on table)* This is where we need accounting sophistication! Extraordinary items! Below the line! One-time, unusual, and infrequent! This gradient theme is a once-in-a-lifetime expense! It should go below the line!

**Jerry:** You bought a WordPress theme. That's not extraordinary. That's a normal business expense for someone who runs a website. Which you don't.

**Kramer:** It's extraordinary because I'll never buy it again! Once you have a gradient, you have it forever!

**George:** He's got a point. If it's not recurring, maybe it's below the line. But what about the hosting? That's recurring. That's above the line—operating expense.

**Jerry:** You two are trying to classify expenses on a blog that has generated $0 revenue and four readers. Three of whom are your mothers.

**Kramer:** The classification matters, Jerry! Extraordinary items are net of tax! They don't affect operating income! If I put the gradient below the line, my operating loss looks smaller!

**George:** Exactly! We need to present our best face to potential investors!

**Jerry:** You have potential investors? Who? Newman?

**George:** *(Hesitating)* ...My father asked how the blog was doing. I told him it was a "pre-revenue growth venture." He said he might invest if he sees potential.

**Kramer:** See? Investor interest! So we need to make our operating loss look as small as possible! Push everything extraordinary below the line!

**Jerry:** You have one expense. A website theme. That's not extraordinary. That's just a bad purchase.

**Kramer:** What about the hats I used for the blog photos? Those were inventory that I converted to promotional use! That's an extraordinary write-down!

**Jerry:** You took hats out of your apartment, photographed them, and put them back. That's not a write-down. That's Tuesday.

---

**Setting:** Monk's Diner. George and Kramer have spread papers across the booth. Jerry sits opposite, amused.

**George:** Okay, I've classified our expenses. Operating expenses: hosting, domain, coffee we bought while writing, and a "consulting fee" I paid myself for content strategy.

**Jerry:** You paid yourself a consulting fee?

**George:** I'm a consultant! I consult on content! It's legitimate!

**Kramer:** What about the gradient?

**George:** That's extraordinary. One-time. Below the line.

**Kramer:** And the hats I used for the photo shoot? I'm declaring a $500 loss on hat impairment due to "sudden change in business model." Extraordinary!

**Jerry:** You took three hats out of a pile of 5,000. The business model didn't change. You just picked them up and put them down.

**Kramer:** It's about intent, Jerry! The intent changed! Before, they were inventory. Now, they're promotional assets! That's a reclassification event!

**George:** Also, I'm categorizing my emotional distress as an "exceptional item" above the line. My father's constant criticism is a significant cost of doing business with a family-owned emotional structure.

**Jerry:** You're putting your dad's comments on the income statement? As an exceptional item?

**George:** It's material! It affects my ability to generate content! Without the emotional distress, I'd be much more productive!

**Kramer:** That's above the line! Exceptional but operating! Good classification, George!

**Jerry:** This is the worst financial statement I've ever seen. And I've seen Kramer's hat balance sheet.

*(Newman arrives, sliding in.)*

**Newman:** Extraordinary and exceptional items. A topic near and dear to my heart. Last year, I had to classify the loss of my entire Beanie Baby collection.

**Jerry:** That was extraordinary? A fad ending?

**Newman:** Extraordinary? No. That was exceptional. Above the line. Because I was in the Beanie Baby business as a regular trade. The loss was part of my operating activities.

**Kramer:** So what's extraordinary then?

**Newman:** An earthquake destroying your warehouse. A flood wiping out your inventory. A government expropriation of your assets. Something truly unusual and infrequent. A gradient theme? That's just stupidity. It's an operating expense.

**George:** What about my father's criticism?

**Newman:** *(Stares at George)* That's a recurring expense. Possibly a permanent fixture of your operating environment. Not extraordinary. Not even exceptional. Just... overhead.

**Jerry:** Overhead. I like that. "Costanza Overhead."

**George:** So I can't classify anything as extraordinary? All my disasters are just... regular business?

**Kramer:** There has to be something! What about the time Newman's dog ate one of my hats? That's unusual and infrequent!

**Newman:** That dog passed away three years ago. The hat incident was a one-time occurrence. You might argue it was extraordinary.

**Kramer:** See! I have an extraordinary item! I'm gonna restate last year's financials! Move the dog-related hat loss below the line!

**Jerry:** You're restating financials that don't exist to reclassify a loss that you never recorded from an event that happened three years ago involving a dead dog. This is a new level of accounting.

**George:** I need my own extraordinary item. Something so bizarre it can't be considered normal business.

**Kramer:** You could set something on fire.

**George:** Deliberate? That's fraud. It has to be unexpected.

**Kramer:** You could... accidentally set something on fire.

**George:** That's still fraud if I do it on purpose.

**Jerry:** You two are now discussing insurance fraud as a way to get a better income statement classification. This is where I get off the ride.

*(Jerry gets up. Kramer grabs his arm.)*

**Kramer:** Wait, Jerry! You're an extraordinary item in my life! Can I list you as a below-the-line deduction?

**Jerry:** No.

**Kramer:** What about as an exceptional item? Above the line?

**Jerry:** You can list me as a "note to the financial statements: this man is not involved in any way and does not endorse these practices."

---

**Setting:** Kramer's apartment. One week later. George and Kramer are reviewing their new financial statements. The apartment is messier than usual, with a scorch mark on one wall.

**Jerry:** *(Entering cautiously)* What happened here? Did you actually set something on fire?

**George:** No, no. That was... an extraordinary event. Unusual and infrequent.

**Kramer:** The toaster had a disagreement with a hat. It was a one-time thing. We've moved the toaster to a different room.

**Jerry:** You burned a hat in a toaster? That's not extraordinary, that's just stupid. It's an operating expense of being Kramer.

**George:** We recorded it as extraordinary. Below the line. Along with the gradient theme and the dead dog hat from three years ago.

**Jerry:** So your extraordinary items now include a WordPress theme, a hat that a dead dog chewed, and a toaster fire.

**Kramer:** And the hat I used for the blog photo shoot! That's four extraordinary items!

**Jerry:** You have four extraordinary items? That's not extraordinary anymore. That's just your regular business. If you have four "one-time" events in one month, they're not one-time. They're just your operating reality.

**Newman:** *(Appearing in the doorway)* He's right. Extraordinary items are supposed to be rare. You've created a category for your own incompetence and called it extraordinary. The SEC would have a field day with this.

**George:** Fine! What if we reclassify the toaster fire as exceptional? Above the line, but separately disclosed? Then it's part of operating profit but highlighted!

**Kramer:** Can we do that? Just move it up?

**George:** It's accounting! We can do anything if we put a footnote!

**Jerry:** The footnote would read: "We burned a hat in a toaster because we are idiots." I don't think that clarifies anything.

**Newman:** The real problem is you have no operating profit to speak of. Whether you put losses above or below the line, they're still losses. You're just rearranging deck chairs on a sinking ship.

**Kramer:** But the deck chairs look better when arranged properly!

**George:** Exactly! Presentation matters! When my father sees these financials, I want him to see that our operating loss is manageable, and our extraordinary losses are just... extraordinary!

**Jerry:** Your father is going to see a blog that made no money, spent $80 on a gradient, and burned a hat in a toaster. There's no presentation that makes that look good.

**Kramer:** Unless... what if we present it as performance art? Then the losses become investments in art! That's a different asset class entirely!

**George:** Performance art! With financial statements as the art! It's meta, Jerry! It's brilliant!

**Jerry:** You're going to convince your father that losing money on a blog and burning hats is performance art? Frank Costanza? The man who invented Festivus?

**George:** Festivus started as a joke! It became a tradition! This could be the same!

**Kramer:** The "Costanza-Kramer Performance Art & Financial Commentary"! It's not a failed blog, it's a conceptual piece about the absurdity of commerce!

**Newman:** You're reclassifying your entire existence as a performance art piece to avoid admitting you failed at blogging. That's... actually a very sophisticated accounting move. Conceptually.

**Jerry:** It's not accounting at all! It's just lying with extra steps!

**George:** It's creative accounting, Jerry! There's a difference!

**Kramer:** So our new income statement: operating loss—performance art investment. Extraordinary items—none, because everything is part of the art. Exceptional items—the gradient theme, because gradients are exceptional by nature.

**Jerry:** You've now put the same expense in three different categories depending on how you feel that day.

**Kramer:** Flexibility, Jerry! That's the key to modern accounting!

**George:** I'm gonna call my father. Tell him we're pivoting to conceptual art. He always said I should be more creative.

**Jerry:** He meant get a different job. Not invent a new category of failure.

*(George exits, dialing. Kramer follows, already discussing gradient aesthetics as art. Newman stays.)*

**Newman:** You know, in a way, they're not wrong. If they truly believe it's art, they could get a grant.

**Jerry:** You think there's a grant for burning hats and writing bad blogs?

**Newman:** Stranger things have been funded. The NEA once gave money to a man who photographed himself eating a phone book.

**Jerry:** Did he also have a deferred tax asset?

**Newman:** *(Smirking)* That's the real art. The tax returns.

*(Newman exits. Jerry looks around the hat-and-scorch-mark apartment.)*

**Jerry:** *(To himself)* Above the line, below the line—they're just drawing lines in the sand. And then burning them. With toasters.
