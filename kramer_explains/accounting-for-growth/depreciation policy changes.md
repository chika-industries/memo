### Scene 24: Changing Depreciation Policy

**Setting:** Jerry's apartment. George is hunched over a laptop. Kramer bursts in with a ledger.

**Kramer:** George! I've found it! The secret to instant profitability!

**George:** Me too! Look at this—my Buick Skylark, I've been depreciating it over five years. But I just changed the useful life to twenty years!

**Kramer:** Genius! My espresso machine—I extended its life from five years to fifty. Depreciation expense just plummeted! My net income skyrocketed!

**Jerry:** You changed the useful life of a coffee maker to half a century? It'll be in a museum by then.

**George:** That's the beauty, Jerry! Depreciation is a non‑cash charge. It doesn't affect cash flow, only reported profits! I just made my Buick look profitable on paper without spending a dime!

**Kramer:** The big corporations do this all the time! They stretch asset lives, change depreciation methods, even adjust residual values. Instant earnings boost!

**Jerry:** So you're just... pretending your assets last forever?

**George:** It's not pretending! I'm aligning useful life with reality. That Buick will outlive me.

**Jerry:** It hasn't moved in three years. It's already dead.

**Kramer:** That's the treadmill effect, Jerry! You boost profits this year, but next year you need even bigger changes to keep the growth going. It's a non‑cash treadmill to nowhere!

**Jerry:** And you're proud of this?

**George:** We're playing the game! My coffee expense—also capitalized it. Then extended its useful life. My profit margins are up 400%!

**Kramer:** I revalued my hats! Increased residual value—now they're worth something at the end of their life. No depreciation at all!

**Jerry:** You can't increase residual value on hats you can't sell. They're not assets, they're decorations in Newman's garage.

**Newman:** *(Appearing in doorway)* I heard "residual value." As managing member of Kramerica Hat Holdings, I'm changing the depreciation policy on the hat inventory from straight‑line to double‑declining balance.

**Kramer:** That accelerates depreciation! That lowers profits!

**Newman:** Precisely. I want lower profits for tax purposes. You two are inflating profits for ego purposes. I'm optimizing.

**Jerry:** So Kramer and George are cooking the books to look rich, and Newman is cooking the books to look poor. This is a complete accounting circus.

**George:** It's called earnings management! Everyone does it!

**Kramer:** We're just... managing.

**Jerry:** You're managing yourselves into a corner. Next year you'll need even longer lives or even higher residual values just to show the same profit. Eventually your espresso machine will have a hundred‑year life and a residual value of $1,000. It's a treadmill to absurdity.

**Kramer:** *(Pauses)* ...So we need to acquire new assets. More espresso machines. More hats. Then we can apply the same aggressive policies to them!

**George:** Fresh assets! Fresh depreciation games!

**Newman:** You have no cash for new assets.

**Kramer:** We'll borrow! Against our increased profits!

**Jerry:** Your increased paper profits. Which you created by changing assumptions. Which means you're borrowing against nothing.

**George:** That's leverage! We learned that!

**Jerry:** You learned nothing.

*(Kramer grabs his ledger, George grabs his laptop.)*

**Kramer:** We're going to the bank! Show them our new profit margins!

**George:** And our extended useful lives! They'll be impressed by our long-term thinking!

**Jerry:** They'll be impressed by the security guard who escorts you out.

*(Kramer and George exit. Newman lingers.)*

**Newman:** Should I tell them the bank will ask for cash flow statements, not adjusted depreciation?

**Jerry:** Let them find out. It'll be a better scene.

*(Newman nods and exits. Jerry sighs.)*

**Jerry:** A treadmill to nowhere. At this rate, they'll be depreciating their own lives over ninety years with a residual value of "maybe."
