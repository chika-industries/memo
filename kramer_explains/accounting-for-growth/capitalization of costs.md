
### Scene 22: Capitalization of Costs

**Setting:** Jerry's apartment. Kramer is holding a large, shiny espresso machine. George is holding a receipt.

**Kramer:** Jerry, I've made a major acquisition. This is a $1,200 espresso machine. Top of the line. But I'm not expensing it—I'm *capitalizing* it!

**Jerry:** You bought an espresso machine. For your apartment. Where you don't even have a kitchen table.

**Kramer:** It's a long-term asset! I'm putting it on my balance sheet and depreciating it over five years. That way, my income statement doesn't take the hit all at once!

**George:** I'm doing the same thing! I bought new tires for my Buick. $400. I'm capitalizing them as a betterment—extending the useful life of the vehicle!

**Jerry:** You're capitalizing tires? On a car that doesn't run?

**George:** It *could* run! These tires might get it there! It's a capital improvement!

**Kramer:** George gets it! You capitalize costs that provide future benefit. This espresso machine will give me future economic benefit every morning for years!

**Jerry:** It will give you caffeine. That's not a capital asset. That's just coffee.

**Newman:** *(Appearing in doorway)* Capitalization rules are strict. A $1,200 espresso machine in a home kitchen is a personal expense. Not depreciable. The tires on a non-operational vehicle? Also not capitalizable. You're both confusing accounting with wishful thinking.

**Kramer:** What about my gradient theme for the blog? $80—I capitalized that as a website development cost!

**Newman:** Website development costs can be capitalized only during the application development stage. You bought a template. That's an expense.

**George:** So everything I buy is just... an expense?

**Jerry:** Welcome to life. Your tires are an expense. Your espresso machine is an expense. My sandwich I just ate—also an expense. The only thing you two are capitalizing is bad decisions.

**Kramer:** *(Deflating)* So I just... write it off?

**Jerry:** No, you don't write it off either. You just pay for it. That's what normal people do.

**George:** Then why do companies get to capitalize everything?

**Kramer:** Because they have accountants! We need accountants!

**Jerry:** You need a budget. And maybe a therapist. But not an accountant.

*(Kramer slumps, holding his espresso machine. George stares at his tire receipt.)*

**Newman:** If it makes you feel better, the postal service capitalizes its mail trucks. But they deliver mail. What do you deliver, Kramer?

**Kramer:** ...Hats. Once.

**Newman:** Then capitalize nothing.

*(Newman exits. Kramer and George sit in defeated silence.)*

**Jerry:** Look on the bright side. You now know the difference between an asset and an expense. That's more than you knew yesterday.

**Kramer:** What's the difference?

**Jerry:** An asset gives you future value. An expense gives you coffee.

**Kramer:** *(Looking at espresso machine)* This gives me coffee. So it's an expense.

**George:** And my tires? They just sit there.

**Jerry:** Expense.

**George:** So my whole life is just one long expense?

**Jerry:** Now you're getting it.
