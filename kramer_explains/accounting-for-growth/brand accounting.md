### Scene 23: Brand Accounting

**Setting:** Jerry’s apartment. Kramer stands proudly with a piece of paper. George is next to him, equally smug.

**Jerry:** What now? You’ve capitalized your espresso machine, off-balanced your hats—what’s next?

**Kramer:** Brand accounting, Jerry. The big boys do it. In the 1980s, companies like Nestlé and Philip Morris bought up food brands and put them on the balance sheet for millions. My brand—Kramer—has value. I’m recording it as an intangible asset.

**Jerry:** Your brand? You’re a guy who yells “Giddy up!” and falls through doors. That’s not a brand. That’s a noise complaint.

**George:** I’m doing it too! My “Costanza” brand has significant value. I’m capitalizing it on my personal balance sheet. I’m thinking $500,000.

**Jerry:** Half a million dollars? For a guy who once ate an éclair out of the trash?

**George:** That was a branding moment! Relatability! Authenticity!

**Kramer:** See, the takeover boom proved it. When you buy a company, the brand is often worth more than the factories. I’m not selling Kramerica—I’m just putting a number on what it’s worth if I *did* sell.

**Jerry:** Who’s going to buy the Kramer brand? What does it even include?

**Kramer:** The name, the image, the hair, the catchphrases, the residual goodwill from years of being me. It’s an intangible asset, Jerry. It doesn’t need to be physical.

**George:** I’ve already had an offer. My father said he’d pay me $100 to stop using the Costanza name.

**Jerry:** That’s not a takeover premium. That’s hush money.

**Newman:** *(Entering)* Brand accounting is a slippery slope. The only reason brands got on balance sheets was because of acquisition accounting—you could only record them if you bought another company. You can’t just conjure a value for your own brand out of thin air.

**Kramer:** Says who? I’m my own acquirer! I’m acquiring the Kramer brand from myself! It’s a leveraged buyout of my own identity!

**Jerry:** You’re buying yourself from yourself? How does that work?

**Kramer:** I’ll borrow money from Newman, use it to buy the brand from me, and then record the brand as an asset and the loan as a liability. It’s pure finance!

**Newman:** I’m not lending you money to buy yourself.

**George:** What if I sell my brand to Kramer, and Kramer sells his brand to me? Then we both have an acquisition and we can both record brand assets!

**Jerry:** That’s just swapping names. You’d still be you. George would still be George. Nothing changes except you have a piece of paper saying you’re worth more.

**Kramer:** Exactly! That’s how the 80s worked! All those conglomerates traded brands back and forth and everybody’s balance sheet looked great!

**Newman:** Until the goodwill write-offs came. Then it all collapsed.

**Jerry:** So your brilliant plan is to recreate the 1980s takeover bubble in your living room, with two brands worth nothing and a lender who hates you both.

**Kramer:** *(Pausing)* ...We need a bigger lender.

**George:** My father?

**Kramer:** No. Someone with deep pockets. Someone who believes in intangible value.

**Newman:** You’re thinking of the post office? We don’t finance vanity assets.

**Kramer:** *(Ignoring him)* What if we take the brand public? Issue shares in “Kramer Inc.”? Sell them to the public?

**Jerry:** The public doesn’t want shares in a man who stores hats in Newman’s garage.

**George:** I’ll buy some shares! For $10! Then you have an arms-length transaction to support the valuation!

**Kramer:** George buys 1% of Kramer Inc. for $10. That values the whole company at $1,000. That’s my brand value!

**Jerry:** A thousand dollars? I thought you said half a million.

**Kramer:** That was George. Mine is $1,000. Modest. Realistic.

**George:** I’m not selling my brand for a thousand. My brand is worth at least a hundred thousand.

**Kramer:** Then you buy my brand for a thousand, and I’ll buy your brand for a hundred thousand. We swap, record the assets, and both look rich!

**Newman:** That’s circular trading. It’s fraud.

**Jerry:** It’s not fraud if it’s so stupid nobody would believe it.

**Kramer:** *(Ignoring)* So George buys Kramer brand for $1,000. I buy Costanza brand for $100,000. But I don’t have $100,000.

**George:** You borrow it from Newman!

**Newman:** I’m out.

*(Newman exits.)*

**Jerry:** So your brand accounting scheme just collapsed because Newman won’t lend you a hundred thousand dollars to buy George’s brand, which is worthless, using Kramer’s brand as collateral, which is also worthless.

**Kramer:** It’s a liquidity problem, Jerry. The underlying assets are solid.

**Jerry:** The underlying assets are your names. You can’t sell them. You can’t borrow against them. You can’t even give them away. They’re not assets. They’re just... you.

**George:** *(Dejected)* So my brand is worth nothing?

**Jerry:** It’s worth whatever your father will pay you to stop using it. Which is $100. That’s your brand value. And it’s not an asset—it’s a liability you owe your father for the privilege of being you.

**Kramer:** What about my brand? I haven’t gotten any offers.

**Jerry:** That’s because nobody wants it. You’d have to pay someone to take it.

**Kramer:** Negative brand value? Is that possible?

**Jerry:** You’re living proof.

*(Kramer slumps. George stares at the floor.)*

**Kramer:** The 80s were so much easier. You just bought a cereal company and suddenly you had a billion-dollar brand.

**Jerry:** You don’t have a cereal company. You have a half-empty jar of pickles and a dream.

**George:** I’m going to go call my father. See if the $100 offer is still on the table.

**Kramer:** That’s not brand accounting. That’s brand liquidation.

**George:** At this point, I’ll take it.

*(George exits. Kramer looks at his paper with his “Kramer” brand valuation, then crumples it.)*

**Kramer:** You know what the real intangible asset is, Jerry?

**Jerry:** What?

**Kramer:** Friendship.

**Jerry:** You can’t put friendship on a balance sheet.

**Kramer:** That’s why my balance sheet will never be accurate.

*(He exits. Jerry shakes his head.)*

**Jerry:** He’s right about one thing—some things you can’t account for. Unfortunately, everything they try to account for also can’t be accounted for.
