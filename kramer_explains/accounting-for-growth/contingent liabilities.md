
### Scene 20: Contingent Liabilities

**Setting:** Jerry's apartment. George is lying on the couch, staring at the ceiling. Jerry is at the kitchen counter, making a sandwich.

**George:** Jerry, I'm in trouble. I've been lying awake thinking about all the things that could go wrong. The blog. The t-shirts. The time I told my boss I was "working from home" while I was actually at a Yankees game. What if they all come back to haunt me? What if I owe money I don't even know about?

**Jerry:** You're having a contingent liability nightmare. It's like a regular nightmare, but with more footnotes.

**George:** Contingent liability? What's that?

**Jerry:** It's a potential obligation. Something that might happen in the future depending on some event. Like, if you're being sued, you might have to pay. But you don't know yet. So you disclose it in the footnotes. It's a "maybe we owe money" situation.

**George:** *(Sitting up)* I have those! I have so many of those! My entire life is a contingent liability!

*(Kramer bursts in, holding a legal-looking envelope.)*

**Kramer:** Jerry! George! This is it! The big one! I've been waiting for this moment!

**Jerry:** What's in the envelope? A lawsuit from the Gradient Theme company?

**Kramer:** Better! It's a contingent asset! Someone might owe *me* money!

**George:** A contingent asset? That's the opposite of my entire existence!

**Kramer:** Remember when that lady hired us to arrange hats on her wall? She loved it! She's been showing her friends! Now, one of her friends is "thinking about" hiring us for her entire penthouse! It's not certain, Jerry, but it's probable! I can recognize a contingent asset on my balance sheet!

**Jerry:** You cannot recognize a contingent asset because someone *might* hire you. That's not a liability or an asset. That's a hope.

**Kramer:** GAAP allows for recognition when the future economic benefit is probable and measurable! I've measured it! $2,000!

**Jerry:** You have a verbal maybe from a friend of a client and you're booking $2,000 as an asset?

**Kramer:** That's how Enron did it!

**Jerry:** That's how Enron went to jail!

**George:** *(Panicking)* Kramer's booking contingent assets, and I'm here with contingent liabilities everywhere! The IRS might audit me! My father might demand repayment of the $600! The Yankees might sue me for wearing a counterfeit jersey! I need to start disclosing these!

**Jerry:** You're not a public company, George. You don't have to disclose anything. You just have to live your life.

**George:** But what if I am a public company? What if "George Costanza" is a publicly traded entity on the exchange of life? I have a duty to my shareholders!

**Jerry:** Your shareholders are your parents. They already know you're a disaster. No disclosure needed.

---

**Setting:** Monk's Diner. George has a notebook labeled "Contingent Liabilities: George Costanza (Parent Company)." Kramer is adding to his own notebook.

**George:** Okay, I'm listing all my contingent liabilities. Number one: the IRS. Probability: possible. Potential exposure: $2,000. Number two: Frank Costanza, personal loan. Probability: probable. Potential exposure: $600 plus interest. Number three: the Yankees. Probability: remote. Potential exposure: $50.

**Jerry:** The Yankees are not going to sue you over a jersey you bought from a guy in a parking lot.

**George:** It's remote, Jerry! I'm disclosing it! That's what good companies do!

**Kramer:** I'm listing my contingent assets! Number one: penthouse hat arrangement. Probability: probable. Potential benefit: $2,000. Number two: Newman's repayment of the Beanie Baby loan. Probability: possible. Potential benefit: $500.

**Jerry:** Newman owes you money? Newman?

**Kramer:** From the Beanie Baby days. He borrowed $500 for a Princess Diana bear. Never paid me back. But he *might*. It's possible!

**Jerry:** That's not a contingent asset. That's a receivable that's past due and unlikely to be collected. You should be writing that off, not booking it as a maybe.

**Kramer:** I'm optimistic, Jerry! Newman's a man of his word!

*(Newman arrives, sliding in.)*

**Newman:** I heard my name. And the phrase "man of his word." Both are accurate.

**Jerry:** Kramer just listed your $500 debt as a contingent asset.

**Newman:** *(Laughing)* That's not contingent. That's uncollectible. I'm not paying you, Kramer. I spent that bear money on a rare stamp that turned out to be a forgery. That debt is a loss, not an asset.

**Kramer:** *(Crossing out in notebook)* Fine. Removing Newman. But the penthouse job is still probable!

**George:** Newman, you're an expert on liabilities. What about my contingent liabilities? Am I disclosing enough?

**Newman:** George, your problem isn't disclosure. Your problem is that your contingent liabilities are more likely than not to become actual liabilities. The IRS? Probable. Your father? Probable. The Yankees? Remote, but you're right to disclose. I'd add one more: potential lawsuit from the t-shirt company you stole the slogan from.

**George:** I didn't steal it! "I'm not a Costanza" was original!

**Newman:** There's a company in New Jersey called "Costanza's Costumes." They might take issue.

**George:** *(Writing frantically)* Adding! Potential exposure: $10,000!

**Jerry:** You're making up numbers now.

**George:** It's an estimate! That's what you do with contingent liabilities! You estimate the range!

**Kramer:** I'm adding one more contingent asset! The hat market might turn around! If it does, my inventory is worth $50,000! That's a contingent gain!

**Jerry:** That's not a contingent asset. That's called "hope." You cannot book hope.

**Kramer:** Warren Buffett books hope! He's hopeful about America!

**Jerry:** Warren Buffett books investments. You book hats. There's a difference.

---

**Setting:** Jerry's apartment. A few days later. George is on the phone, looking pale.

**George:** *(Into phone)* Yes, I understand. Yes, I'll be there. Thank you.

*(He hangs up, staring blankly.)*

**Jerry:** What happened? Did the IRS find you?

**George:** Worse. My father.

**Jerry:** Frank? What did he do?

**George:** He's asserting his claim on the $600. He's called a family meeting. He's calling it "The Costanza Repayment Tribunal." He's bringing witnesses.

**Jerry:** Witnesses? To a family loan?

**George:** My mother, my cousin, and the guy who saw me borrow the money in 1998. That guy is now a notary.

**Kramer:** *(Bursting in)* George! Bad news! The penthouse lady's friend hired someone else! My contingent asset is gone! It's a contingent nothing!

**George:** At least you lost something you never had. I'm about to lose something I actually owe!

**Jerry:** So both of your contingencies just resolved. George's probable liability became an actual liability. Kramer's probable asset became a definite nothing.

**Kramer:** That's the nature of contingencies, Jerry! They're uncertain! That's why they're not on the balance sheet!

**George:** But they should be in the footnotes! I'm putting this whole family meeting in my footnotes! "Subsequent event: The Costanza Repayment Tribunal occurred. The company expects to pay $600 plus emotional damages."

**Kramer:** I need new contingent assets! Something more reliable!

**Newman:** *(Appearing at the door)* You want reliable? I'll give you a reliable contingent liability. The post office is being sued by a carrier who slipped on a wet floor. If they lose, they might pass costs down to us. I might have to contribute to a settlement fund. That's a contingent liability.

**Kramer:** For you? That's bad. For me? That's nothing.

**Jerry:** Newman, why are you telling them this?

**Newman:** Because they need to understand that real contingent liabilities are about actual risks, not hat markets and family loans. You two are playing at accounting. Real companies have real exposures.

**George:** I have real exposures! My father is a real exposure!

**Newman:** Frank Costanza is not a material exposure. He's a nuisance claim at best.

**Jerry:** I'm gonna tell Frank you said that.

**Newman:** *(Quickly)* Don't.

**Kramer:** So what do I do? I have no contingent assets and my only contingent liability is Newman's Beanie Baby debt which he says is uncollectible. I'm contingency-free!

**Jerry:** You have a toaster that's burned a hat, 5,000 hats that nobody wants, and a gradient theme that cost $80. You're not contingency-free. You're just unaware of your contingencies.

**George:** Like what?

**Jerry:** Like the fact that you have hats stacked to the ceiling and your landlord hasn't seen your apartment in six months. That's a contingent liability—possible eviction. Like the fact that you used a toaster in a way that violates fire codes. That's a contingent liability—possible fine. You're not free, Kramer. You're just not thinking ahead.

**Kramer:** *(Slowly)* So I'm... contingent?

**Jerry:** You're the most contingent person I've ever met. Your entire existence is a footnote waiting to happen.

**George:** That makes two of us.

**Newman:** Three, actually. The post office contingency could affect me. I need to start my own footnote file.

**Jerry:** This is now a support group for people who want to disclose their potential disasters. I'm gonna go make a sandwich that has no contingent anything.

*(Jerry exits to the kitchen. George, Kramer, and Newman sit in silence.)*

**George:** What's the proper disclosure for "I'm afraid of everything"?

**Kramer:** I think that's called "forward-looking statements." They always say those are uncertain.

**Newman:** You'd need a whole separate document for that. It's called a life.

---

**Jerry:** *(From kitchen)* I can hear you! And I'm updating my own footnote: "Company has no affiliation with these individuals and assumes no liability for their financial ignorance."
