### Scene 16: Treasury Stock

**Setting:** Kramer's apartment. The hat situation is slightly better—meaning there are paths now. Kramer is on the phone, looking serious.

**Kramer:** No, no, Newman, it's not illegal! It's treasury stock! Companies do it all the time! ... What do you mean, "with what money"? I'll explain later!

*(He hangs up as Jerry enters cautiously.)*

**Jerry:** I see you've created walking paths. Very civilized.

**Kramer:** Jerry! You're just in time! I'm about to execute a major financial maneuver! Treasury stock repurchase!

**Jerry:** You're going to buy back your own stock? What stock? You don't have stock. You have hats that people don't want.

**Kramer:** I'm creating stock! Kramerica Hat Industries is going public! Well, not public public. More like... private public. But I need to buy back some shares!

**Jerry:** Why would you buy back shares of a company that has no revenue, no profits, and no future?

**Kramer:** To increase the value of the remaining shares! It's basic finance! When you reduce the number of shares outstanding, each share becomes more valuable! I'm making my existing shareholders richer!

**Jerry:** Who are your shareholders?

**Kramer:** *(Proudly)* Me! And Newman! I sold him 100 shares at $1 each last week!

**Jerry:** You sold Newman stock in your hat company? And he paid you?

**Kramer:** $100! He believed in the vision! But now I realize I gave away too much of the company. I need to buy back those shares—treasury stock!—so I can regain control!

**Jerry:** You want to buy back shares from Newman with what money?

**Kramer:** That's the beauty part! I'll use the $100 he gave me! I'll just give it back to him!

**Jerry:** So you're going to give Newman back his money, and in exchange, he'll give you back the worthless shares, and you'll be exactly where you started except you'll have accomplished... nothing?

**Kramer:** I'll have *treasury stock*, Jerry! It's an asset on my balance sheet! Shares that were issued and then reacquired! It shows I have confidence in my own company!

**Jerry:** It shows you had $100, gave it to Newman, got it back, and now you're calling that "treasury stock." That's not an asset. That's a circle.

*(George enters, looking confused.)*

**George:** Kramer, I've been thinking about what you said. Treasury stock. Is that like... when the government keeps money in a big chest somewhere?

**Kramer:** No, no! It's corporate finance! A company buys its own shares from shareholders! It reduces outstanding equity!

**George:** So if I'm a shareholder in myself—like, I own shares of George Costanza—I could buy back shares of me from other people?

**Jerry:** Who owns shares of you? Other than your parents, who wish they could sell?

**George:** That's the thing! My ex-girlfriend Susan's parents—before she, you know, passed—they still have some claim on me emotionally! Like they own part of my guilt! If I could buy back those shares, I'd be free!

**Kramer:** Exactly! Treasury stock of the soul! You're buying back the emotional equity!

**Jerry:** You two have officially lost the thread. Treasury stock is a real thing, but you're both describing something completely different. Kramer is trying to give Newman his money back to feel important, and George wants to pay his dead girlfriend's parents to stop feeling guilty. That's not accounting. That's just sad.

**George:** It's not sad! It's a buyback! A repurchase of distressed equity!

**Kramer:** He's right! We're both just trying to optimize our capital structure!

**Jerry:** Your capital structure is a mess. Kramer has hats and $20. George has $4,100 and a Buick that doesn't run. There's no structure. There's just... stuff.

**George:** *(Ignoring Jerry, to Kramer)* How much would you charge to help me structure this buyback? I need a financial advisor.

**Kramer:** For you, George, 50 shares of Kramerica Hat Industries. They're currently trading at... *(looks around, picks a number)* ...$2 a share!

**George:** So I give you $100 worth of advice credit, and I get 50 shares?

**Kramer:** Done! We'll do a private placement!

**Jerry:** You're going to advise George on his emotional buyback, and he's going to pay you in imaginary stock of a company that Newman just got refunded from.

**Kramer:** It's complex, Jerry. You wouldn't understand.

**George:** Yeah, Jerry, this is sophisticated finance. Let the professionals work.

*(George and Kramer huddle together, whispering and nodding seriously.)*

**Jerry:** *(To himself)* I'm surrounded by people who think a circle is an asset. And they're gonna advise each other. This is how financial crises start.

---

**Setting:** Monk's Diner. One week later. George and Kramer sit dejected. Jerry watches with amusement.

**Jerry:** So how did the treasury stock buyback go?

**George:** Terrible. I went to Susan's parents with a proposal to buy back their emotional equity. I offered them $500.

**Jerry:** How'd they react?

**George:** Her mother cried. Her father threw me out. Said I was trying to "commodify grief." I don't even know what that means but it felt bad.

**Kramer:** My buyback didn't go well either. Newman refused to sell.

**Jerry:** Why would he refuse? You were offering his own money back!

**Kramer:** He said the shares are now worth more because I've shown confidence by trying to buy them back. He wants $5 each! I've created a higher valuation just by making an offer!

**Jerry:** So you increased your own stock price by trying to repurchase shares you sold for $1, and now you can't afford to buy them back.

**Kramer:** It's a classic squeeze, Jerry! I'm being squeezed by Newman! With my own money!

**George:** At least you have money to be squeezed. I'm down $500 and I still have the guilt.

**Jerry:** You know what you two need?

**George:** What?

**Jerry:** A write-off. Just write the whole thing off. Emotional equity, treasury stock, all of it. Claim it as a loss on your mental tax return.

**Kramer:** *(Brightening)* Wait... can we do that?

**Jerry:** You can do anything if you don't understand accounting. And you two are the undisputed champions.

*(Kramer and George look at each other, then nod slowly.)*

**Kramer:** George, I think we just found our next financial strategy.

**George:** Write-offs all around!

**Jerry:** And there it is. Full circle. We're back to where we started. The write-off that never writes off.
